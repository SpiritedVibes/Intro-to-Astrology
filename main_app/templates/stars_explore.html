Star Life Cycle

Stage 1: Protostar

A star begins its life as a protostar, which forms from a cloud of gas and dust. Over time, the gas and dust compress under gravity, heating up until nuclear fusion begins.

Stage 2: Main Sequence

During this phase, stars burn hydrogen into helium in their cores through nuclear fusion. This is the longest phase of a star's life cycle, and our Sun is currently in this stage.

Stage 3: Red Giant

Once a star exhausts its hydrogen fuel, it swells into a red giant or supergiant. In this stage, the star starts fusing heavier elements, such as helium, into carbon.

Stage 4: Supernova

For massive stars, when nuclear fusion stops, the core collapses, leading to a supernova explosion. This explosion can create a neutron star or black hole, depending on the mass of the star.

Stage 5: Neutron Star or Black Hole

If the star is massive enough, the supernova leaves behind a black hole. If it's not as massive, it will form a neutron star, which is incredibly dense and composed mostly of neutrons.

Iron's Role in Star Death

In the final stages of a massive star's life, the core begins to fuse iron. Unlike other elements, iron does not produce energy when fused, and this leads to core collapse and the eventual formation of a black hole or neutron star.

Hypernova

A hypernova is an extremely powerful supernova caused by the collapse of an extremely massive star. It can produce a black hole and release more energy than a regular supernova.

Neutron Stars vs. Black Holes

The key difference between a neutron star and a black hole is that a neutron star has a physical surface, while a black hole does not. The event horizon of a black hole marks the point beyond which nothing, not even light, can escape.

Life Span of Stars

Stars with greater mass have shorter life spans, as they burn through their fuel faster. Low-mass stars can last billions of years, while massive stars may only last a few million years before they explode as supernova.

The Event Horizon

The event horizon is the boundary surrounding a black hole beyond which nothing, not even light, can escape. Once something crosses this point, it is lost to the black hole forever.
